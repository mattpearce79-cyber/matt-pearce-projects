The brief, in one line

Redesign the top nav, balancing Twinkl's breadth with a manageable, easy, reliable experience.

Twinkl currently offers 1.5M+ resources across K–12, every subject, every region, and every age group. The current top nav is asked to be a search starting point, a category browser, a region switcher, and a subscription upsell — all at once.

Audit · existing top nav

Six things asking for the user's attention at once.

1 2 3 4 5 6
  1. 1 Competing CTAs. Logo, £0.00 Twinkl Trial, Join for FREE all shout for the click. Three primary buttons within a 200 px stretch.
  2. 2 Lost utility. School Enquiries, the language flag, and Sign In sit between the loud CTAs — quietly important but visually dismissed.
  3. 3 No label on language. The flag is the entire affordance — accessible only if you recognise St George's Cross.
  4. 4 Disney link clashes. The brand chip is rendered in exactly the colour the other nav items adopt on hover — users mistake it for a "selected" state.
  5. 5 Dual-function nav items. Hover opens a mega menu; click takes you to a landing page. Same target, two outcomes, no signposting.
  6. 6 Hover delay reads as a dead control. The reveal lag (~200 ms) makes users believe the chip didn't respond and they click — pre-empting the menu.

What we observed

  • 3 / 3 users clicked the nav item rather than waiting for hover.
  • 2 / 3 said unprompted: “Usually I'd expect to click the navigation item.”
  • 2 / 3 were thrown by Disney looking like a “selected” state.
  • 2 / 3 noticed the rollover delay and clicked through it, missing the menu.
  • 3 / 3 commented on the number of buttons in the header.
The pattern is unanimous: hover-to-reveal does not match expectation for this audience. A click-revealed mega-nav (or both) is the safer default.

Insights

A user's first question is almost never “what subject?”

01

Age first, subject second.

Most session starts begin with "I'm teaching Year 1 phonics today" — a year-group context the user already holds. Subject and resource type narrow from there.

02

Search is the real front door.

Power users type the exact resource title (Twinkl's SEO-strong page names exist because of search-led entry). Nav is a fallback when search fails or for new users browsing.

03

The nav is also a merchandiser.

"Twinkl Recommends" exists because Twinkl ships big products (Originals, Phonics, Rhino Readers) that don't fit a single subject column. The nav needs a place for products, not just topics.

04

Region locks taxonomy.

England's KS1/KS2 ≠ Scotland's Early Level / First / Second. A nav that surfaces age stages by name only works after the locale is set.

Three solutions we shipped

From muddled to deliberate.

1

Two rows, one job each.

We split the top chrome into two stacked rows: brand · search · account on top, and browse + audience below. Each row owns a single job, so search isn't fighting three CTAs for the click.

Two ways to browse — by age, or by subject.

Instead of mixing age stages, product silos, and brand chips on the same row, we promoted two clear browse modes. Leaders, Parents, and the Twinkl Store each get a dedicated spot.

Solution · 01 · Desktop · resting state · low fidelity

A two-row chrome with one job per row.

Desktop · top nav · resting · low fidelity
Row 1 — utility. Logo, dominant search, two small chips, sign-in, and one pink CTA. Nothing competes for the same click.
Row 2 — browse. "Browse by age", "Browse by subject", Leaders, Parents, Twinkl Store. Clear taxonomies, one per item.
Disney earns its place as a featured partner chip — visually distinct from a hover state, never confused for "selected".

Solution · 02 · Desktop · "Browse by age" open · low fidelity

Click reveals an age rail and three orthogonal columns.

Click, not hover. The chip toggles the panel — matching what every user in research expected.
Stages on the left, facets across. Early Years, KS1‒KS4–5, SEND & Inclusion, Home Education all stay one tap away.
"Twinkl Recommends" reclaims a column instead of being a 13-row dump. "Browse all" escape hatch sits at the bottom.

Solution · 03 · Mobile · low fidelity

Same logic, vertical. Search persists, browse collapses into a labelled list.

Resting

  • 2

  • 3

  • 4
    Featured content stays below the panel. The menu opens as a sheet over content, not as a dead end.

How we'd know it worked

Four signals, in priority order.

1

Time to first resource view

From a cold landing to opening any resource detail page. Target: −15% at the 75th percentile within 6 weeks.

2

Nav-to-search ratio

Share of sessions that only use search vs. those that touch the nav. Healthy nav = ratio shifts toward nav-assisted browsing.

3

Mega-nav abandonment

Hovers that close without a click. Today this isn't measured; we'd add it as the primary nav-health metric.

4

Featured-card CTR

Tracks whether the "earned" slot is doing its job — and feeds editorial back.

Validation: a 4-week A/B with a 10% holdout, gated by an unmoderated tree test before launch to catch new-taxonomy confusion early.
