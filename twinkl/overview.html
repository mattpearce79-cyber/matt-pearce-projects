Overview

Redesigning the top navigation for 1.5 million resources and 6 million users.

Twinkl's top navigation serves over 6 million educators, parents, and school leaders browsing a catalogue of 1.5 million resources. The existing nav collapses too many audiences, taxonomies, and commercial goals into a single flat row, producing competing calls to action and a hover-triggered mega menu that research shows users consistently bypass. This project redesigns it around clear intent-based categories and a click-first interaction pattern.

 

  • Collapse 12+ mixed nav items into five intent-based categories covering age, subject, audience, and commercial destinations
  • Replace the hover-triggered mega menu with a click-first interaction that matches observed user behaviour
  • Improve time to first resource with a two-panel mega menu that lets users navigate by curriculum stage without leaving the nav
  • Validate the redesign through moderated usability testing, benchmarked against task-completion times on the existing site

Pain points

01

Competing calls to action

The logo, a purple trial button, and a pink join button all sit within a 200px stretch of the header, each demanding the same click. Users are forced to evaluate three primary actions before they have even started browsing.

02

Utility links are visually dismissed

School Enquiries, the language selector, and Sign In are sandwiched between loud CTAs. They carry real user value but receive no visual hierarchy, making them easy to miss entirely.

03

No label on the language selector

The country flag is the only affordance for region switching. It is accessible only to users who recognise the flag, excluding international visitors and anyone unfamiliar with UK regional flags.

04

Disney chip reads as a selected state

The Disney partnership chip is styled in the same colour that standard nav items adopt on hover, causing users to interpret it as the currently active section rather than a featured partner link.

05

Dual-function nav items with no signposting

Hovering over a nav item opens the mega menu; clicking navigates to a category landing page. The same target produces two different outcomes with no visual indication of which action does what, creating consistent confusion in user testing.

06

Hover delay reads as a broken interaction

The mega menu reveal delay of approximately 200ms leads users to believe their hover did not register. All three research participants clicked through the delay, navigating away from the nav before the menu had a chance to open.

07

Overlapping age ranges cause ambiguity

The age categories 3–5 and 5–7 share the boundary year, leaving users uncertain which group a 5-year-old belongs to. Without clarification, parents and teachers may browse the wrong category or check both, adding unnecessary friction to a task that should be immediate.

Assumptions

01

Disney button is a commercial priority

The Disney partnership chip is assumed to represent a high-priority commercial relationship that must be preserved and given prominent placement in any redesign. All design decisions treat this as a fixed constraint rather than an element open to removal or de-emphasis. If this assumption is incorrect, the Disney chip could be absorbed into the standard nav taxonomy like any other content category.
