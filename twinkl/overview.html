Twinkl Nav Redesign

Senior / Lead Product Designer

The Brief Pain Points Research Design Outcomes

27th May 2026

Pain Points

Heuristic review of the current navigation across desktop and mobile

A heuristic review of the existing Twinkl navigation identified pain points across four areas: the header, navigation structure, mega menu, and mobile experience.

Header

01

Competing calls to action

The logo, a purple trial button, and a pink join button all sit within a 200px stretch of the header, each demanding the same click. Users are forced to evaluate three primary actions before they have even started browsing.

02

Utility links are visually dismissed

School Enquiries, the language selector, and Sign In are sandwiched between loud CTAs. They carry real user value but receive no visual hierarchy, making them easy to miss entirely.

03

Personalisation control has no label

The flag icon is the only affordance for what is actually a full personalisation control. Clicking it opens a modal to set Country, Region, Language, and Career, and the navigation structure changes based on those selections. The icon gives no indication of this scope, and without a text label the control has no accessible name, failing WCAG 1.1.1 and 2.4.6 at Level AA.

Navigation Structure

01

Disney chip

The Disney partnership chip is styled in the same colour that standard nav items adopt on hover, causing users to interpret it as the currently active section rather than a featured partner link.

02

Dual-function nav items

Hovering over a nav item opens the mega menu; clicking navigates to a category landing page. The same target produces two different outcomes with no visual indication of which action does what, creating consistent confusion in user testing.

03

Hover delay

The mega menu reveal delay of 250ms leads users to believe their hover did not register. All three research participants clicked through the delay, navigating away from the nav before the menu had a chance to open.

04

Overlapping age ranges

The age categories 8–11 and 11–16 share a boundary year. Without clarification, users are left uncertain which group an 11-year-old belongs to, adding unnecessary friction to a task that should be immediate.

Mega Menu

01

Content truncates

Items such as Topics and Events and Festivals are clipped at the bottom of the mega menu. A small arrow is present but easy to miss, and many users will not attempt to scroll because the affordance does not clearly signal that more content exists below.

02

Content is mixed

The first column of the mega menu (Twinkl Recommends) is a promotional editorial list, not a navigation category. Users encounter marketing copy before they reach any subject or age content, breaking the expected pattern of a nav menu. Link labels compound this: entries like “WIN! A space on a CPD course for free, over 70+ to choose from!” sit alongside single-word links like Phonics and Writing, making the column slow to scan and visually inconsistent with the rest of the menu. User research reinforced this: participants described the Twinkl Recommends column as distracting and felt it was “in the way” when trying to locate specific content categories.

Mobile

01

Mobile navigation

  • The mobile nav is a horizontally scrolling strip with no scroll indicator or fade. Items such as Age 8–11 onwards are visibly truncated on the right with no affordance to suggest more exist.
  • The Disney chip retains its prominent first position on mobile, appearing before Home and all age categories.
  • The Join CTA sits in the header alongside Sign In and the flag, competing for attention in a constrained space.
  • The flag-only language selector persists on mobile with the same accessibility issue as desktop, with no text label and no accessible name for screen readers.
  • Nav items in the strip appear small and closely spaced, potentially falling below the 44×44px minimum touch target size recommended by WCAG 2.5.5.
